Introduction

The Final Fantasy Pixel Remaster series is a vital preservation project that brings the franchise's foundational 8-bit and 16-bit classics to a new audience. For those of us old school gamers who grew up in the 90's, it cannot be overstated how the company Squaresoft shaped the role-playing genre. What the Pixel Remaster brings is a revitalization of the classic mechanics that shaped the genre afterwards. By faithfully recreating the original pixel art with enhanced resolution, music arranged with real instruments, and better dialogue for storytelling, players can now experience on the newer consoles the games that laid the foundations for a series that continues to entertain players, 40 years later.

A Brief History

The early Final Fantasy games emerged in the late 1980s during a booming but fiercely competitive Japanese RPG scene, defined largely by the popularity of Dragon Quest. Square’s Final Fantasy was initially seen as a last-ditch effort to save the struggling company, and its success sparked a healthy rivalry with Dragon Quest’s developers at Enix, pushing both series to innovate in storytelling, combat, and world-building. Director Hironobu Sakaguchi shaped the foundation of Final Fantasy by emphasizing cinematic narrative, emotional stakes, and a more dramatic sense of adventure than most RPGs of the time. Composer Nobuo Uematsu gave the series its soul through unforgettable music, using the limited hardware of the Famicom to craft themes like the Prelude, Victory Fanfare, and Main Theme that became synonymous with the franchise’s identity. Artist Yoshitaka Amano contributed ethereal character designs and iconic logo art that set Final Fantasy apart visually, giving each game a mythic, dreamlike atmosphere. Through the first six entries, this trio’s collaboration helped define Final Fantasy’s core DNA—emotional stories, experimental gameplay systems, and striking art and music—in constant dialogue and competition with Dragon Quest, ultimately raising the standard for RPGs worldwide.
